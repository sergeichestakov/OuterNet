On 21 February 2017, at 8:59 am local time, a Beechcraft King Air aircraft operating a charter flight, carrying a pilot and four passengers bound for King Island , crashed just after taking off from Essendon Airport in Melbourne , the capital of the Australian state of Victoria. [2]

Accident [ edit ]

The pilot made two mayday radio calls that preceded the ground impact [3] before the aircraft clipped the roof of a building housing the Essendon Direct Factory Outlets (DFO) then impacted the ground a few metres away and ignited, starting a fire. [4] [5] The Direct Factory Outlets complex, located on the airport grounds, had not yet begun the day's trading. [1] All five people on board were killed in the crash, including the four passengers who were American tourists on their way to King Island to play golf. [5] Victorian Premier Daniel Andrews said it was the worst civil aviation accident in Victoria in 30 years. [6]

The aircraft was reported by media sources to have crashed as a result of an engine failure on take-off. [5]

Some local residents and aviation groups have stated that the crash shows that buildings have been constructed too close to the airport for safety. [7] [8] [9]

The official investigation's initial findings include that there were no pre-existing aircraft faults, that the pilot made a mayday call, but the voice recorder failed and that witnesses said the take-off took "longer than normal". Engine tear-downs conducted show that both powerplants were free to rotate and both propellers showed evidence of rotation, although only the left propeller left slash damage to the building roof. Further findings will be made public as the investigation is concluded. [10] [11]

Aircraft [ edit ]

The aircraft was a Beechcraft B200 King Air , serial number BB-1544 and registered as VH-ZCR; it was manufactured in 1996. [2] [12]

Aftermath [ edit ]

Smoke plume from the crash site

Speaking to media representatives, the Executive Director of the Australian branch of the Aircraft Owners and Pilots Association (AOPA) criticised the practice of allowing commercial development of land at Australian airports such as the Essendon Direct Factory Outlets complex, saying that these reduced the options available to pilots in emergencies. He stated that AOPA had been calling on governments for two decades to stop such developments going ahead. [13] A spokesman for the Australian Federation of Air Pilots also criticised commercial development of land at Essendon Airport. [14] Anti-airport campaigners renewed calls for the airport to be closed altogether. [14]
