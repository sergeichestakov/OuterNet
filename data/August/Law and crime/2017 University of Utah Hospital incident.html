2017 University of Utah Hospital incident

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Jump to: navigation , search

On July 26, 2017, Jeff Payne, a detective for the Salt Lake City Police Department (SLCPD), arrested the nurse Alex Wubbels at the University of Utah Hospital after she refused to draw blood from an unconscious patient. Footage of the incident released on August 31, 2017 went viral. The SLCPD announced policy changes which would affect how police should handle situations involving drawing blood, and the hospital announced it would change its police protocol to avoid repeating the incident.

History [ edit ]

Incident [ edit ]

On July 26, 2017, Marcos Torres, a pickup truck driver fleeing from Utah Highway Patrol troopers in Cache County, Utah , crashed head-on into a semi-truck. Torres was killed, and William Gray, the semi-truck driver, was severely burned. [1] Gray was taken into the University of Utah Hospital in a sedated and comatose state. [2]

Released footage of the arrest incident show a dispute between police detective Jeff Payne and the nurse Alex Wubbels over obtaining a blood sample from Gray. Wubbels argued that this situation would not meet the legal requirements allowing her to take a blood sample from an unconscious patient. [1] Payne put the nurse on the phone with his lieutenant, who reportedly told her she could be arrested if she refused to provide the sample. Wubbels continued to refuse, and Payne arrested her. [3] Wubbels was later released and no charges were brought against her. [2] Gray later died on September 25, 2017. [4] [5]

Investigations [ edit ]

In September 2017, the Salt Lake County District Attorney Sim Gill announced that his office is investigating the arrest. [6] Gill has requested investigative assistance from the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI). [7] FBI spokeswoman Sandra Yi Barker said the agency opened a civil rights review after the video went public, has agreed to help the county investigate the incident, [8] and has opened a probe into the arrest. [9] The Unified Police Department of Greater Salt Lake is also investigating into the arrest. [10]

On September 13, 2017, Jackie Biskupski , the mayor of Salt Lake City, said that an internal investigation revealed that Payne and his watch commander James Tracy violated six staff policies during the incident, including policies regarding arrests and standards of conduct. [11] A report from Salt Lake City's Police Civilian Review Board also alleged that Payne and Tracy violated police policies. It stated that Payne became upset and frustrated during the incident, eventually "[losing] control of his emotions." Payne's lawyer said this report was based on speculation. [12] [13] On September 25, the Salt Lake Police Association, a police union in Utah, criticized the city for its treatment of the police officers involved in the incident. [14]

Response [ edit ]

According to a statement posted by Salt Lake City mayor Jackie Biskupski , the SLCPD launched an internal affairs investigation within 24 hours of the July 26 incident, meeting with the hospital's CEO and nursing management team. [10] After the incident, the SLCPD changed its policies on drawing blood. A police department spokeswoman said these changes require law enforcement to have consent or a warrant to draw blood, instead of just implied consent . [15] According to hospital officials, within weeks after the incident they had implemented new rules meant to allow patient care staffers to focus on their work and minimize disputes in patient care units. [16] The hospital announced these changes changes in September 2017, including not allowing police officers in patient-care areas and having them speak with "house supervisors" instead of nurses. [17]

At a press conference on August 31, 2017, Wubbels and her attorney released footage of the incident taken from hospital surveillance and police body cameras. [18] The footage of the incident soon went viral online. [19] The SLCPD faced heavy criticism on social media for how it handled the incident. [20] The SLCPD apologized to Wubbels for the arrest, [21] and put Payne on paid leave on September 1, 2017. [22] Another police officer connected to the incident was put on paid leave the next day. [23] According to Payne's attorney, Payne has expressed a desire to apologize to Wubbels for the incident. [24]

On September 2, about a hundred Utahns gathered at a Utah Against Police Brutality rally near the SLCPD courtyard to call for Payne to be fired. [25] The University of Utah police chief apologized to Wubbels on September 4, saying that the university police should have done more to protect her on the night she was arrested. [19] Payne was fired from his part-time job as a paramedic for Gold Cross Ambulance on September 5. [26]

On September 20, the Utah State Legislature 's Judiciary Interim Committee voted unanimously to start drafting a bill meant to clarify consent laws regarding police-ordered blood draws. [27]
