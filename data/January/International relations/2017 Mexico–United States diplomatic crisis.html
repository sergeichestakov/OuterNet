The 2017 Mexico–United States diplomatic crisis is a diplomatic stagnation between both North American nations due to the recent friction between their current presidents. It began when the President of the United States Donald Trump , threatened Mexico with renegotiating the North American Free Trade Agreement because he considers it not to be beneficial to his country, and also said that he would amplify the barrier that already exists between both countries; Mexican President Enrique Peña Nieto said he opposed both proposals. Later, Donald Trump and Enrique Peña Nieto canceled a meeting they had scheduled in Washington D.C. [1]

Background [ edit ]

Since the Republican candidate Donald Trump entered into the presidential race on June 16, 2015, one of his main campaign promises was the creation of a wall on the southern border to make illegal immigration more difficult for people attempting to illegally enter the United States from Mexico . During his campaign he promised his supporters that the US government would build the wall and Mexico would pay for it. After Donald Trump became the Republican presidential nominee, this had become an important issue in the presidential campaign. In response, the Mexican government, under President Enrique Peña Nieto has stated that Mexico would not pay for the new border wall. The Trump administration looked into the possibility of adding a new tax to Mexican imports to help fund the construction and maintenance of the new border wall. [2]

Current status [ edit ]

As of the end of September 2017, Congress has not appropriated funding for a wall, and no further wall construction has started beyond what was already planned during the Obama administration. [3] Since the middle of May, the effort by the White House to pursue building a wall appears to have been put on hold, at least temporarily, as the administration has been spending more time defending Trump from allegations of inappropriately dismissing FBI director James Comey , and carelessly disclosing classified information to Russia . [4]

Timeline of the crisis [ edit ]

January 20 [ edit ]

On his inauguration speech, Donald Trump said "We will bring back our jobs", proclaiming he will put America first and that they will follow two simple rules: buy American and hire American. [5]

Enrique Peña Nieto congratulated Trump on his inauguration and promised Mexicans that he would protect them as he seeks a respectful dialogue with the Trump administration and relations that will be beneficial for Mexican citizens. [6]

January 22 [ edit ]

US President Donald Trump announced he will begin renegotiating the North American Free Trade Agreement when he meets with the leaders of Canada and Mexico. Mexican President Peña Nieto scheduled a meeting on Washington D.C. on January 31. [7]

January 23 [ edit ]

Mexican President Peña Nieto listed 10 goals Mexico will seek in the negotiations, including a commitment by the US to respect the legal rights of Mexican citizens and safeguarding the free flow of remittances, which amount to about $25 billion per year. He said "Mexico does not believe in walls, but in bridges". [8]

January 25 [ edit ]

President Donald Trump signed Executive Order 13767 , which mandates that a physical wall be constructed along the southern border of the US. [9]

January 26 [ edit ]

President Trump wrote a tweet where he said "If Mexico is unwilling to pay for the badly needed wall, then it would be better to cancel the upcoming meeting." President Peña made a video where he said "I have said it over and over again. Mexico will not pay for any wall". After that, the Mexican President canceled the meeting he had planned with President Trump. [10] Washington journalist Dolia Estévez , who was interviewed in Aristegui Noticias, said she got access to part of a one-hour phone conversation that happened between the two presidents the day of the supposed meeting; she stated "Trump humillated Peña Nieto" and that the conversation only lasted 20 minutes but was prolonged to an hour because it was made through translators. [11] [12] [13]

February 24 [ edit ]

The Mexican government stated they will not under any circumstances accept foreign nationals from third countries that the United States wants to deport. In recent years the majority crossing from Mexico into the United States have been from Central America. [14]
