North Carolina is currently divided into 13 congressional districts , each represented by a member of the United States House of Representatives . After the 2000 Census , the number of North Carolina's seats was increased from 12 to 13 due to the State's increase in population.

Constitutionality of the 2010 redistricting [ edit ]

On February 5, 2016, a three-judge panel of U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit and U.S. District Court for the Middle District of North Carolina judges ruled that the 1st and 12th districts' boundaries were unconstitutional and required new maps to be drawn by the legislature to be used for the 2016 election. [2] On May 22, 2017, the U.S. Supreme Court, in Cooper v. Harris , agreed that the 1st and 12th congressional district boundaries were unlawful racial gerrymanders, the latest in a series of cases dating back to 1993 by different parties challenging various configurations of those districts since their first creation. [3] [4]
